Guillotine Generator

Iterative area-weighted edge-to-edge splits.

Example Boards (15×15)

How It Works

The Guillotine generator uses an iterative splitting loop. It starts with the full grid and repeatedly picks the largest rectangle (area-weighted random selection) and cuts it in half with an edge-to-edge line.

  1. Start with one rectangle covering the entire N×N grid
  2. Find all splittable rectangles (area > 1)
  3. Pick one using area-weighted random selection (larger = more likely)
  4. Choose direction: prefer the longer dimension (1.5× threshold), else random
  5. Choose cut position: uniform random along the chosen axis
  6. Replace the rectangle with its two halves
  7. Repeat until the target clue count is reached: round(N^1.4)

Why Area-Weighted Selection?

Area weighting ensures the largest rectangle is always the most likely to be split next. This naturally balances sizes вҖ” large rectangles keep getting subdivided while small ones are left alone. The result is a relatively uniform distribution: many medium-sized rectangles with few extremes.

Key Characteristics

  • Uniform-ish sizes: most rectangles cluster around the average area
  • High rectangle count: N^1.4 clues (e.g., ~152 for 40×40)
  • Clean structure: every partition has edge-to-edge guillotine lines
  • Fast: simple iterative loop, no recursion
  • Deterministic count: always produces exactly the target number of rectangles
Comparison with Natural: The Guillotine generator produces many more, smaller, more uniformly-sized rectangles. The Natural generator produces fewer rectangles with a much wider range of sizes (heavy-tailed distribution). Both use guillotine cuts, but the iterative vs recursive approach creates very different visual feels.
